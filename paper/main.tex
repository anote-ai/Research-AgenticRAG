\begin{abstract}
Agentic retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems interleave retrieval,
reasoning, and answer generation across multiple hops.  This makes failures
difficult to diagnose: a retrieval error at hop 1 can surface only as a wrong
answer at hop 3, while later retrieval may partially repair the trajectory.  We
introduce \ours, a benchmark and experimental framework for causal failure
attribution in agentic RAG.  \ours{} logs hop-level traces, injects controlled
faults at specific hops, re-runs the downstream suffix of the agent trajectory,
and evaluates diagnosers against certified intervention labels.  Across
HotpotQA, MuSiQue, FRAMES, and CRAG, with Claude, GPT-4o-mini, and local/open
model conditions, we find a sharp identifiability collapse for coverage-based
post-hoc diagnosis: mean hop-1 attribution accuracy is 0.93, but mean
depth-2/3 accuracy falls to 0.30 across 14 real-backbone conditions.  A
counterfactual propagation-aware localizer improves some narrow MuSiQue
settings but does not consistently beat the best post-hoc baseline at exact-hop
localization, winning only 3 of 13 deep conditions.  However, an LLM-as-judge
diagnoser is comparatively depth-robust, and counterfactual recovery rates show
that agents often absorb injected retrieval faults.  These results suggest that
failure propagation is a first-class evaluation axis for agentic RAG, and that
current local repair probes need stronger causal signals than single-hop
evidence replacement.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) improves factual question answering by
conditioning generation on retrieved evidence rather than only on model
parameters \citep{lewis2020rag}.  Recent agentic variants make retrieval
iterative: a language model chooses sub-queries, observes retrieved documents,
and decides whether to search again or answer, in the style of ReAct-like
reasoning and acting \citep{yao2022react}.  This extra agency is useful for
multi-hop questions, but it also changes the failure mode.  The observed error
at the end of a trajectory is often not the root cause.

Consider a three-hop RAG agent.  If hop 1 returns irrelevant evidence, hop 2 may
drift into a wrong sub-problem, and the final answer may be ungrounded or
incorrect.  A post-hoc evaluator that only inspects the final trace can label
the answer as wrong, or identify a late low-coverage hop, while missing the
earliest causal fault.  Conversely, some agents recover: a bad hop can be
absorbed by later retrieval and never appear as an end-to-end failure.  Standard
accuracy, retrieval recall, and answer correctness do not distinguish these
cases.

This paper studies \emph{failure propagation}: how faults injected at a
particular hop or stage move through an agentic RAG trajectory, whether the
agent absorbs them, and whether diagnostic methods can recover the certified
root cause.  The accompanying repository implements \ours, a provider-agnostic
agentic RAG benchmark with static and live interventions, hop-level diagnostic
metrics, and experiment runners for real LLM backbones.

Our current findings are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Controlled live intervention is feasible.}  The benchmark
  corrupts a trajectory prefix at hop $h$ and then re-runs the suffix with the
  same agent, producing causal labels and genuine downstream reactions rather
  than deterministic post-hoc edits.

  \item \textbf{Attribution identifiability collapses with propagation depth.}
  A coverage-gated \dr-style baseline is nearly perfect at hop 1, but falls
  sharply at depths 2 and 3.  Across 14 real-backbone conditions, mean accuracy
  drops from 0.93 at hop 1 to 0.30 at deeper hops.

  \item \textbf{The current counterfactual localizer is not yet a general win.}
  Our \pa{} method beats the best post-hoc baseline in only 3 of 13 deep
  conditions.  Dense retrieval, a larger probe budget, and larger samples did
  not remove this gap.

  \item \textbf{Recovery is substantial and dataset-dependent.}  Counterfactual
  recovery is low on FRAMES, moderate on MuSiQue, and high on HotpotQA in dense
  OpenAI runs, showing that end-to-end failure rates alone hide both fragility
  and robustness.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{RAG and active retrieval.}
RAG combines a parametric generator with external non-parametric memory
\citep{lewis2020rag}.  Follow-up work studies when and what to retrieve,
including active retrieval during long-form generation \citep{jiang2023flare}
and self-reflective retrieval decisions \citep{asai2023selfrag}.  These methods
aim to improve answers; our work instead treats retrieval and reasoning errors
as interventions and measures where they propagate.

\paragraph{Agentic RAG diagnosis.}
ReAct-style agents interleave reasoning and external actions
\citep{yao2022react}, which creates trajectories that can be inspected,
localized, and repaired.  \citet{jiao2026doctorrag} propose a diagnose-and-
repair framework for agentic RAG with coverage-gated localization and prefix
reuse.  Our \dr{} baseline captures this coverage-gated localization idea, but
our evaluation asks a causal question: when the true injected root cause is
known, how often does any diagnoser recover it as propagation depth increases?

\paragraph{Multi-hop and RAG benchmarks.}
HotpotQA \citep{yang2018hotpotqa} and MuSiQue \citep{trivedi2021musique}
provide multi-hop QA structure.  FRAMES \citep{krishna2024frames} evaluates
factuality, retrieval, and reasoning in end-to-end RAG; CRAG
\citep{yang2024crag} stresses dynamic and long-tail factual QA.  We use these
datasets as substrates for injected fault trajectories rather than as only
answer-accuracy benchmarks.

\paragraph{Causal evaluation.}
The intervention view follows the language of causal inference
\citep{pearl2009causality}: rather than observing a bad trace and guessing what
caused it, \ours{} performs $do(\mathrm{failure}=f, \mathrm{hop}=h)$ and scores
diagnoses against the known intervention.

\section{Task and Trace Model}

Each example is a question-answer pair with a retrieval corpus.  An agent emits
a trace
\[
  \tau = (q, \{q_h, D_h\}_{h=1}^{H}, A, y, c),
\]
where $q$ is the user question, $q_h$ is the sub-query at hop $h$, $D_h$ is the
set of documents retrieved at hop $h$, $A$ is the final answer, $y$ is the
reference answer, and $c$ is token cost.  The repository implements this as
\texttt{PipelineTrace}, with flattened retrieved documents, tool calls, hop
queries, hop documents, iteration count, and token usage.

Failures are labeled by stage:
\[
  s \in \{\mathrm{retrieval}, \mathrm{tool}, \mathrm{answer}, \mathrm{none}\}.
\]
Retrieval failures include empty retrieval, irrelevant retrieval, query drift,
false-premise evidence, stale evidence, and early termination.  Answer failures
include empty answers, incorrect answers, and hallucinations.  A diagnostic
record stores a predicted stage, failure type, propagation flag, severity, and
root-cause string.

\section{Interventional Benchmark}

\subsection{Agent}

The real-agent runner, \texttt{LLMAgent}, implements a provider-agnostic
ReAct-style loop.  At each hop the provider emits either
\verb|{"action": "search", "query": ...}| or
\verb|{"action": "answer", "answer": ...}|.  The same loop supports Claude,
OpenAI-compatible models, and a deterministic mock provider.  Retrieval is
pluggable: the experiments use BM25, token-overlap, and dense
\texttt{sentence-transformers} retrieval through one interface.

The important implementation detail is resumability.  Given a prefix of
executed hops, the agent can resume from hop $h+1$ and continue reasoning.  This
makes live failure injection possible.

\subsection{Static and Live Interventions}

The static injector edits a completed trace.  This is useful for deterministic
unit tests and ablations, but it cannot measure how an agent reacts to a fault.
The live injector instead constructs a corrupted prefix and re-runs the suffix.
Table~\ref{tab:interventions} summarizes the live interventions.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}

Intervention & Stage & Operationalization \\
\midrule
Empty retrieval & Retrieval & Replace $D_h$ with $\emptyset$ and resume \\
Irrelevant docs & Retrieval & Replace $D_h$ with off-topic noise documents \\
Query drift & Retrieval & Replace $q_h$ with a drifted sub-query, retrieve, resume \\
False premise & Retrieval & Prefix evidence with a confidently wrong premise \\
Stale evidence & Retrieval & Prefix evidence with outdated temporal evidence \\

\bottomrule

\end{table}

\subsection{Diagnosers}

We compare four diagnosers.

\paragraph{\rb.}
A rule-based wrapper around \texttt{DiagnosticBenchmark}.  It detects empty
retrieval, missing tool calls, empty answers, hallucinations by grounding
overlap, and incorrect answers.  It sees only the final trace.

\paragraph{\dr.}
A coverage-gated localizer inspired by \dr{}.  It identifies the earliest hop
whose documents have insufficient token overlap with the gold answer, and
otherwise attributes the failure to answer generation.

\paragraph{\lj.}
An LLM-as-judge diagnoser that reads the hop-level trace and predicts the
earliest failing stage and hop.  With the mock provider it falls back to a
deterministic coverage heuristic; with real providers it incurs token cost.

\paragraph{\pa.}
An active counterfactual diagnoser.  For each hop in causal order, it repairs
that hop's evidence by re-retrieving from the corpus, forces the agent to answer
from the repaired full prefix, and returns the earliest hop whose repair flips a
wrong answer to a correct answer.  It also probes whether continuing retrieval
would repair a premature answer.  The method is explicitly more expensive
because it spends re-execution tokens.

\section{Metrics}

\paragraph{Attribution identifiability.}
For injected traces that still fail after the intervention, we score whether a
diagnoser predicts the correct injected hop:
\[
  \mathrm{Acc}_d(h) =
  \frac{1}{|\mathcal{F}_h|}
  \sum_{\tau_i \in \mathcal{F}_h}
  \mathbf{1}[\hat{h}_{d,i} = h_i],
\]
where $\mathcal{F}_h$ contains injected traces at depth $h$ whose final answer is
incorrect after suffix re-execution.  The main C2 curve is
$\mathrm{Acc}_d(h)$ versus injection depth.

\paragraph{Counterfactual recovery.}
Recovery is the fraction of live interventions whose corrupted trajectory still
produces a correct answer:
\[
  \mathrm{Recovery}(h) =
  \Pr[\mathrm{correct}(A, y) \mid do(f, h)].
\]
Recovered traces are excluded from root-cause scoring because there is no final
failure to attribute.

\paragraph{Cost per correct diagnosis.}
For token-spending diagnosers, we report total tokens divided by the number of
correct localizations.  This separates diagnosis quality from deployability.

\paragraph{Static propagation graph.}
For static ablations, \texttt{PropagationGraph} estimates empirical stage
coupling $P(s_{h+1}=t \mid s_h=s)$, mean propagation depth, critical path, and
self-correction rate.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.}
The repository supports HotpotQA, MuSiQue, FRAMES, and CRAG.  HotpotQA and
MuSiQue serve as anchor multi-hop QA datasets.  FRAMES supplies variable-depth
RAG questions and Wikipedia passage caches.  CRAG contributes dynamic,
long-tail, and false-premise style examples.

\paragraph{Backbones and retrievers.}
The real-backbone runs currently include Claude Haiku 4.5, GPT-4o-mini, and
OpenAI-compatible local models such as Qwen2.5:7B.  Retrieval conditions include
BM25 \citep{robertson2009bm25} and dense retrieval with budget-4
propagation-aware probes.  Static controls use BM25 and token-overlap.

\paragraph{Interventions.}
The headline identifiability experiment injects retrieval-family faults at hops
1, 2, and 3 using empty retrieval, irrelevant retrieval, false premise, and
stale evidence.  For each sample and depth, all interventions are applied to the
same base trace, then the downstream suffix is re-executed.

The tables below summarize results present in \texttt{results/} as of July 1,
2026.  The phase-2 master log did not append a final completion line, but
result JSONs and \texttt{RESULTS\_SUMMARY.md} were regenerated after the latest

\subsection{LLM-Judge is More Depth-Robust than Coverage Gating}

The current results summary reports that \dr{} usually drops sharply from hop 1
to deeper hops, while \lj{} is comparatively flat.  For example, on Claude
MuSiQue dense, \dr{} drops from 1.00 to 0.12 by hop 2, whereas \lj{} rises from
0.16 to 0.76.  On OpenAI FRAMES dense, \dr{} drops from 0.98 to 0.03 by hop 2,
whereas \lj{} remains around 0.64.  This suggests that reasoning over the full
trajectory can be more robust to propagation than simple gold-answer coverage,
although it incurs token cost and can still mislocalize.

\subsection{Static Control: Propagation Masks Retrieval Roots}

The static ablation grid gives a deterministic control over HotpotQA and

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why coverage collapses.}
Coverage gating assumes the earliest low-support hop is the root cause.  Once
the agent re-runs the suffix, later hops can either mask the original fault or
create new low-support evidence.  The final trace no longer carries a reliable
signature of the injected hop.  The observed collapse is therefore an
identifiability limit, not just a weak implementation.

\paragraph{Why the current \pa{} method underperforms.}
The current \pa{} probe repairs one hop at a time and forces an answer from the
modified hop sequence.  This is a strong causal idea but a weak localizer when
multiple downstream artifacts depend on the original fault.  Repairing hop $h$
while holding later corrupted hops fixed may not flip the answer even when $h$
was the true cause.  Conversely, repairing a later answer-bearing hop can flip
the outcome and steal credit from the earlier cause.  This explains the gap
between exact-hop scoring and the looser stage criterion, where \pa{} often
looks much stronger.

\paragraph{Implications for repair.}
The negative C3 result is useful.  It suggests that deployable repair should not
only ask ``which hop flips if repaired?'' but should model paths of downstream
dependence.  Candidate next steps are multi-hop repair sets, structural
counterfactuals that regenerate downstream hops after each repair, explicit
query-drift detection, and confidence-calibrated abstention when several hops
are causally entangled.

\section{Limitations}

First, several result files are partial.  Dense Claude HotpotQA currently
contains only hop 1; dense Claude MuSiQue contains hops 1--2.  Second, scoring
uses exact-hop localization as the strict headline criterion.  Stage-level
diagnosis is easier and yields different conclusions for \pa{}.  Third, the
mock provider is only a control, not a model of real LLM reasoning.  Fourth,
the current LLM-as-judge results are sensitive to prompt design and token cost.
Finally, the dataset adapters rely on available supporting documents or fetched
Wikipedia passages, so corpus construction quality affects both intervention
and recovery rates.  The current runner now records actual injected hops and
skips short base traces by default; older persisted result files should be
rescored with actual-depth filtering or regenerated before making final
depth-specific claims.

\section{Conclusion}

\ours{} turns agentic RAG failure analysis into an intervention problem.  By
injecting faults at known hops and re-running the downstream suffix, it separates
three phenomena that end-to-end accuracy conflates: whether a fault causes a
final failure, whether the agent recovers, and whether a diagnoser can recover
the certified root cause.  The current experiments strongly support the
identifiability-collapse claim and show substantial counterfactual recovery.
They do not yet support a broad exact-hop advantage for the current
propagation-aware diagnoser.  The next version of the method should treat
propagation as a path-localization problem rather than a single-hop repair
probe.
