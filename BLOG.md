## When RAG Agents Fail, Can We Find Out Why?

Agentic RAG systems answer hard questions by searching in multiple rounds, reasoning over what they find, and searching again, which makes their failures genuinely hard to debug: a bad retrieval in the first hop can surface three hops later as a wrong answer, and by then the trace no longer points back to the original mistake. Our research framework, AgenticRAG-FP, turns this into a controlled causal experiment by injecting known faults (empty retrievals, misleading evidence, or a single flipped fact inside an otherwise clean document) at a specific hop, letting the real agent continue from there, and then testing whether diagnostic methods can recover the true root cause. The results, which are still being finalized, show a sharp identifiability collapse: coverage-based diagnosis averages 0.93 accuracy when the fault sits at hop 1 but drops to about 0.30 for deeper faults across 14 model and dataset conditions, and on subtle content corruptions it falls all the way to zero while a counterfactual probing method holds 0.67. We also find that roughly 15% of corrupted facts get repeated verbatim in the final answer, that agents frequently self-correct when the underlying corpus stays clean, and that different diagnostic probes fail in complementary situations, which suggests future systems should choose their diagnostic strategy based on the suspected fault type. Together with a fully deterministic, judge-free evaluation of how corrupted facts spread into generated answers, we hope this makes failure propagation a standard axis for evaluating agentic RAG.
